Background

Packetcraft is a software-focused company, centered on the development of complete and versatile software that can be used by other potential companies. These clients can then combine Packetcraft's software with their own hardware to manufacture their products.

Though Packetcraft itself is not directly involved in the production of end-user merchandise, it still needed a way to demonstrate the capabilities of its software to potential clients, including its Bluetooth LE microphone/receiver software.

My Role

As Associate UI/UX Engineer, I oversaw key design decisions for this microphone/receiver project.

The two main tasks I completed were:

  1. Determine LED specifications of the mic/receiver hardware prototype, and
  2. Create the design for the corresponding demonstrative mobile app.

LED Research

Overall, I focused on various principles of user experience and product design when creating the LED specifications of the mic/receiver hardware prototype.

In summary:

  • I familiarized myself with similar Bluetooth microphones on the market and their LED usage to better understand how to communicate multiple messages effectively
  • I considered color theory when designating colors to certain meanings
  • I accounted for every use case that the mic/receiver software can accommodate to fully display its capabilities

Mobile App Lo-Fi Design

First, I worked on creating some preliminary lo-fi screens covering the key functionalities of Packetcraft's software.

Gathering Feedback

Once I had completed the general structure of the lo-fi prototype, I conducted a couple of user tests on current Bluetooth microphone/headset users. I aimed to test how intuitive it was for users to navigate through the app and perform several key tasks, including successfully pairing a device and upgrading its firmware.

Additionally, I presented my lo-fi prototype to the larger team working on this project in order to gain early feedback from them on the design's current progress.

Key Takeaways

I ended up receiving some very valuable feedback from both my interviews and my team critique.

Feedback from User Interviews:

  • Some in-app instructions, such as device pairing, were not intuitive enough
  • The display of certain device information felt disorganized/unnecessary

Feedback from Team Critique:

  • Important to keep user persona in mind - Packetcraft's customers, as well as end users
  • Distinguish functionality for two main use cases (microphone vs. PA system)

From this, I learned that I needed to focus on clearly defining the target audience. Due to the nature of the app being mostly demonstrative and geared towards other companies, I hadn't been sure to what extent I should consider the end customer in my design. However, after talking to the team, I realized that the app also functions as a way for our clients to determine the look and feel of their own app.

In this way, I saw that it's still important to keep the end user in mind. Understanding this clarified how I should approach the design - simplifying processes and displays for the average Bluetooth microphone user, while still providing all the advanced settings that our clients would be interested in.

Challenges

Working on this project exposed me to new situations as a UI/UX designer and an industry design niche that I was unfamiliar with.

Unlike the type of projects I've worked on in the past, this microphone project was not directly geared towards end-stage target users - in this case, Bluetooth microphone consumers.

This prototype was being made to help demonstrate Packetcraft's software to other companies, so the designs for both the prototype device's I/O and its associated mobile app were not actively geared toward end-microphone users.

Throughout this process, I faced questions such as: how can I construct a design rationale for my work when it's contained within an intermediary stage of a larger context? How should I go about performing human-centered design when my work is situated in such an early stage of product development?

Receiving feedback from the team and user interviews helped me realize that although the context of my work didn't directly involve end users, there is still value in designing with the end user in mind. Business clients viewing this mobile prototype would benefit from an intuitive experience so that they understand the capabilities of Packetcraft's software. Furthermore, a user-centric design can help them consider how to best approach their own product/mobile app, which ultimately ends up with users.

Final Thoughts

Next Steps

If I had more time to work on this project, I would:

  • Conduct additional iterations of user testing on the hi-fi prototype
  • Experiment with different colors/components in the final design
  • Ensure smooth transition from design to code with the app developers

Reflection

Working on this project was a very valuable experience that taught me a lot about designing software prototypes for hardware products. While designing for the intended audience of this prototype was a new and unfamiliar process at first, I learned some important lessons about how user-centered design is vital, even in the early stages, of any product that will eventually find its way to users.

I enjoyed taking ownership of various design decisions made throughout this process, many of which were informed by the crucial feedback I received from my interviewees as well as the rest of the project team. This experience also strengthened my commitment to creating useful and intuitive designs by defining the intended audience clearly and prioritizing all aspects of their perspective in the final product.

Finally, I'd like to thank Packetcraft for the opportunity to work on this project!
